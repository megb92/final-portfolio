Local Food Systems

History

The Matanuska valley was colonized by white settlers from the lower 48 United States during the New Deal era, two decades before Alaska officially became a state. The Matanuska colony established settled agriculture in Alaska with row crops, beef cattle herds, dairy farms, and newly built towns. Colonization effectively ended native Athabascans' use of the region for hunting and gathering.

Throughout Alaska, indigenous Athabascan, Inupiat, Tlingit, Haida, and Aleut peoples continue to live on their ancestral homelands. Remote native villages experience a lack of access to consistent healthcare and public servies. As traditional food production methods have fallen off, tribal members often end up subsisting on preserved processed foods imported from outside the state.

Tribal Food Sovereignty

To counteract unhealthy food systems, several Alaska Native tribal corporations and the Alaska Native Health Center in Anchorage have started sponsoring farmers markets where Native peoples can buy and sell fresh produce. The ANHC also works to serve culturally appropriate foods sourced from the local region to its patients.
